# Limitations — Real-World Evidence Studio

> **Synthetic data disclaimer.** All findings in this application are derived
> from synthetic records. They do not represent real patients, clinical outcomes,
> treatment effectiveness, drug safety, or incidence rates.

---

## 1. Synthetic data

All data is generated by a synthetic patient simulator — either the Synthea
open-source simulator or the bundled custom demo generator. Both produce
longitudinal EHR records that resemble real data structurally but are not
derived from any real individuals.

**Implication:** No descriptive statistic, association, or model output in this
application represents a real population finding. Do not use results to inform
clinical decisions, drug safety assessments, regulatory submissions, or public
health reporting.

## 1a. Additional limitations when using the custom demo generator

The bundled `scripts/generate_demo_data.py` uses hard-coded demonstration
probabilities that are **not** derived from Synthea's disease modules, any
clinical guideline, or any epidemiological dataset.

- Demographic distributions (race, ethnicity, age) are arbitrary demonstration
  parameters, not calibrated population estimates.
- Disease prevalences, comorbidity patterns, and prescribing sequences are
  simplified and do not match Synthea outputs or any real-world data.
- ED outcome probability (~35% follow-up ED rate) is a demonstration target
  chosen to produce a non-trivial regression, not an epidemiological estimate.
- Passing data-quality checks confirms only that the CSV files have the correct
  structure, not that the clinical content is plausible.
- Demo cohort sizes, outcome rates, subgroup findings, and regression
  coefficients must not be presented as expected or representative results.

## 2. GLP-1 prescribing in synthetic data

Synthetic prescribing patterns (whether from Synthea or the custom generator)
do not replicate any real-world prescribing pattern or clinical guideline.
Results cannot be used to compare the real-world effectiveness or utilization
of individual GLP-1 agents.

## 3. Text-based concept matching

Drug and diagnosis identification relies on case-insensitive substring matching
against source description fields and SNOMED codes embedded in the CSV output.
This approach is **not equivalent to validated clinical phenotyping**. In real
RWE studies, concept sets are reviewed by clinical subject-matter experts and
validated against gold-standard cohorts.

**Implication:** Cohort membership in this application reflects the simulation
model used to generate the data, not any formally validated phenotype algorithm.

## 4. New-user design assumptions

The new-user design assumes the first GLP-1 record in the source file is truly
a new initiation. Prior exposure before the observation window cannot be
detected, and synthetic data does not model real pharmacy records that would
be needed to confirm new use.

## 5. Baseline observation window

The 365-day baseline window is approximated by the presence of at least one
encounter record before the index date. Simulation observation periods are
bounded by the simulation start date, so some patients may appear to have
shorter histories than expected.

## 6. Complete-case analysis

Missing values for HbA1c and BMI are common because synthetic data only
generates these measurements when clinically indicated by the simulation logic.
The complete-case logistic regression excludes patients with missing covariates,
which may introduce selection bias even in synthetic data.

## 7. No confounding adjustment beyond regression

The multivariable logistic regression adjusts for observed baseline covariates.
Unmeasured or unmeasurable confounders (provider prescribing patterns, patient
preferences, disease severity gradations not captured in the simulation) are
not modelled. No causal interpretation of the regression coefficients is appropriate.

## 8. Encounter-class classification

Synthetic encounter classes (emergency, inpatient, ambulatory, etc.) are
structurally similar to but not identical to CMS site-of-service definitions
used in real claims-based RWE studies. ED visit counts may not align with
real-world ED utilization research definitions.

## 9. No competing risks

Death is treated as administrative censoring. A competing-risks analysis (e.g.,
Fine-Gray sub-distribution hazard model for the time-to-ED outcome) is not
implemented in V1.

## 10. OMOP demonstration layer

The OMOP-aligned layer uses concept ID 0 for all codes that have not been
mapped through an official OHDSI vocabulary. It is provided for illustrative
purposes only and does not constitute a validated OMOP CDM extract. See
`docs/omop_mapping.md` for details.

## 11. Small-sample instability

With a small source population, the enrolled GLP-1 cohort may be very small
(fewer than 50 patients is common at 500 total). Logistic regression estimates
are highly unstable at this scale, and the application will emit explicit
warnings when diagnostic thresholds are not met. Use at least 1,000–2,000
patients for a more meaningful demonstration.

## 12. Single-simulation data source

Results vary across simulation runs because each is generated with a different
random seed. The application's audit trail captures the exact input file
manifest (name, size, SHA-256 hash) so that results from different runs are
not inadvertently compared.
